Where are people fleeing from?

1992: the ashes of former Yugoslavia

The secession of several states from Yugoslavia in the early 1990s led to a series of wars in Balkan countries such as Croatia, Serbia and Bosnia-Herzegovina. This was the main driver behind the huge numbers of intra-European asylum applicants in 1992.

This year Eurostat has recorded 134,125 applications from citizens of Kosovo, Albania and Serbia so far in 2015, which is a 56.4% increase on the total for 2014. But most asylum applications from these countries today are likely to be rejected as they are all now deemed “safe" by the EU.

2015: wars in the Middle East

There are more refugees from Syria than any other country in the world, according to the United Nations High Commission for Refugees (UNHCR). The majority of the more than four million people fleeing the four years of civil war have found refuge in surrounding states such as Turkey. Despite a new record being broken, Europe’s share of asylum seekers compared with these countries has been relatively small. Ongoing conflicts and political instability in both Iraq and Afghanistan means that many of their nationals are also trying to make it into the EU. There have been 294,280 asylum applications from Syria, Iraq and Afghanistan so far this year, with more than a quarter going to Germany.

Where are people fleeing to?

Asylum applications in 1992 and 2015 compared

Germany

Germany has received more asylum seekers so far in 2015 than any other EU country with over 245,000 applications lodged by the end of August. The German chancellor, Angela Merkel, has spoken on several occasions about the need for other EU member states to share responsibility in welcoming refugees. It also took the most in 1992, receiving just under 440,000 asylum applications.

Hungary

The Hungarian government has built a 4m-high fence along its border with Serbia, a prime migration route for those travelling into Europe. Hungary has had the highest number of asylum applications per capita this year – 15 for every 1,000 residents.

Sweden

Sweden has the second highest number of asylum applications per capita in the EU in 2015 so far with eight made for every 1,000 residents. Over a third of these are by Syrians, who are offered permanent residency if they make it to the Nordic country. Sweden punched above its weight in the previous record year of 1992 too, with around 84,000 asylum applications.

France

France joined with Germany in a successful call for a mandatory redistribution of Syrian, Afghan and Eritrean refugees across EU countries over the next few years. Citizens of those three countries combined have contributed 2,835 asylum applications to France. In 1992, France diverged from other major EU nations with more applications from Asian countries such as Sri Lanka and former colonies in Africa than the crisis-hit European countries.

Italy

In 1991, Italy lodged 18,700 applications for asylum from Albanians, more than seven times the grand total number of applications the country received in 1992. This year over 100,000 people have crossed the Mediterranean attempting to reach Italy's shores, according to the International Organisation for Migration, with thousands dying along the way.

Austria

Earlier this year hundreds of people attempted to walk from Budapest in Hungary across the Austrian border after they were barred from boarding trains. In 2015 so far Austria has received 10,050 asylum applications from Syrians, around 2,000 more than in the whole of 2014.

Belgium

Around a fifth of asylum applications in 1992 to Belgium were from its former colony the Democratic Republic of the Congo. People from the African country only make up a much smaller proportion of this year's total with 435, 10 times fewer than the number of applications by Iraqis.

United Kingdom

David Cameron recently announced that Britain would take 20,000 Syrian refugees over the next five years. Germany received 11 asylum applications for every one received by Britain* between January and July 2015. In 1992 the UK received 32,300 asylum applications.

Romania

Romania is one of the four EU member states that this voted this year against the introduction of quotas to relocate Syrian, Eritrean and Iraqi refugees from Italy and Greece. In 1992, a significant number of Romanians would have been among the thousands of Europeans applying for asylum within the EU.

Methodological note
There are now 28 EU member states, while there were only 15 in 1992. The data from 1992 is not as complete as today's figures, which makes an accurate state-by-state difficult. In several cases only an estimate is possible. Eurostat figures are subject to rounding so sums may not add-up.
