Where are people fleeing from?

1992: the ashes of former Yugoslavia

The secession of several states from Yugoslavia in the early 1990s led to a series of wars in Balkan countries such as Croatia, Serbia and Bosnia-Herzegovina. This was the main driver behind the huge numbers of intra-European asylum applicants in 1992.

This year Eurostat has recorded 134,125 applications from citizens of Kosovo, Albania and Serbia so far in 2015, which is a 56.4% increase on the total for 2014. But most asylum applications from these countries today are likely to be rejected as they are all now deemed "safe" by the EU.

2015: wars in the Middle East

Syria has produced more refugees than any other country in the world, according to the United Nations high commissioner for refugees (UNHCR). The majority of the more than four million people fleeing the four years of civil war have found refuge in surrounding states such as Turkey, vastly outnumbering those seeking asylum in Europe. Nevertheless, there have been 160,655 asylum applications by people from Syria. Iraq and Afghanistan, also suffering from ongoing conflict and instability, have produced 133,615 applicants.

Where are people fleeing to?

Asylum applications in 1992 and 2015 compared

Germany

Germany has received more asylum seekers so far in 2015 than any other EU country. Over 245,000 applications were lodged by the end of August. The German chancellor, Angela Merkel, has spoken on several occasions about the need for other EU member states to share responsibility in welcoming refugees. Germany also received the most applications in 1992 - just under 440,000.

Hungary

The Hungarian government has built a 4m-high fence along its border with Serbia. It is part of a package of hardline measures aimed at reducing the number of refugees and migrants entering the country. Hungary has had the highest number of asylum applications per capita this year - 15 for every 1,000 residents.

France

France joined with Germany in calling for a mandatory redistribution of Syrian, Afghan and Eritrean refugees across EU countries over the next few years. Taken together, citizens of those three countries have made 2,835 asylum applications to France. In 1992, France bucked the trend, receiving more applications from Asian countries and former colonies in Africa than from crisis-hit areas of Europe.

Italy

In 1991, Italy lodged 18,700 applications for asylum from Albanians, more than seven times the grand total number of applications the country received in 1992. This year more than 100,000 people have crossed the Mediterranean attempting to reach Italy's shores, according to the International Organisation for Migration.

Austria

Earlier this year hundreds of people attempted to walk from Budapest in Hungary across the Austrian border after they were barred from boarding trains. So far in 2015 Austria has received 10,050 asylum applications from Syrians, about 2,000 more than in the whole of 2014.

Belgium

About a fifth of asylum applications in 1992 to Belgium were from its former colony the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Congolese people have only made 435 applications this year, 10 times fewer than the number of applications by Iraqis.

United Kingdom

David Cameron recently announced that Britain would take 20,000 Syrian refugees over the next five years. Between January and July 2015 Germany received 11 asylum applications for every one received by Britain* . In 1992 the UK received 32,300 asylum applications.

Romania

Romania is one of the four EU member states that this year voted against the introduction of quotas to relocate Syrian, Eritrean and Iraqi refugees from Italy and Greece. In 1992, a significant number of Romanians would have been among the thousands of Europeans applying for asylum within the EU.

Methodological note
There are now 28 EU member states, while there were only 15 in 1992. The data from 1992 is not as complete as today's figures, which makes an accurate state-by-state difficult. In several cases only an estimate is possible. Eurostat figures are subject to rounding so sums may not add-up. The amount of data available varies by country, with some having filed data for up to the end of July and others having submitted figures for up to and including September.

Source: Eurostat
